# Social Deduction & Role-Based Party Games — Research

Research into simplified Mafia/Werewolf variants and role-playing party games. Focus: understanding the **mechanics** that make these fun, so they can be re-skinned with recovery themes for a treatment center setting. Constraint: must work on a single phone passed around 4-10 men.

---

## Why Classic Mafia Has Problems

Before diving into alternatives, it helps to understand what's wrong with the original:

1. **Player elimination** — You get killed early, you sit and watch for 30+ minutes. Terrible for group engagement.
2. **Requires a dedicated moderator** — One person can't play; they just narrate.
3. **Long game length** — Can drag on 30-60 minutes.
4. **Complex night phases** — Multiple wake-ups, eyes closed, easy to mess up.
5. **No information** — Villagers have almost zero concrete info; it devolves into random accusations.
6. **Violence-themed** — Murder, killing, mafia — not ideal for a recovery/therapeutic context.

Every game below solves at least 2-3 of these problems.

## Tier 1: Best Candidates for Phone-Based Recovery Game Adaptation

These games have mechanics most suited for a single-phone, pass-around format with 4-10 players.

---

### 1. One Night Ultimate Werewolf

**Players:** 3-10 | **Time:** 10 minutes | **Complexity:** Low-Medium

**How It Works:**
- Each player gets a secret role card. Three extra cards go in the center (unclaimed).
- ONE night phase: roles wake up in sequence and perform actions (peek at cards, swap cards between players, etc.).
- ONE discussion period: Everyone opens eyes, talks, lies, accuses.
- ONE vote: Everyone simultaneously points at who they think is the werewolf.
- If a werewolf dies, village wins. If no werewolf dies, werewolves win.

**Key Roles & Night Actions:**
- **Werewolf** — Sees who the other werewolf is
- **Seer** — Peeks at one player's card OR two center cards
- **Robber** — Steals another player's card (swaps with their own), sees their new role
- **Troublemaker** — Swaps two OTHER players' cards (they don't know)
- **Villager** — Does nothing (but that's a role too)

**What Makes It Brilliant:**
- **No elimination.** Everyone plays the whole game.
- **Your role might have changed.** The card-swapping mechanic means you might START as a villager but END as a werewolf and not know it. This creates genuine confusion and hilarity.
- **The app IS the moderator.** The official app narrates the night phase — no human moderator needed.
- **10 minutes per round.** You can play 5-6 rounds in an hour.
- **Discussion is everything.** The entire game is one big conversation where everyone is trying to piece together what happened.

**Why It Works for Recovery Adaptation:**
- App-driven narration = perfect for single phone
- No elimination = everyone stays engaged
- Short rounds = high replay, low stakes per round
- The "your identity might have changed" mechanic is deeply thematic for recovery (who are you really? can you trust your own story?)
- Discussion-centered = builds communication skills

**Phone Feasibility:** HIGH. The official app already works as pass-and-play on one phone. The night phase is narrated by the app while everyone has eyes closed. Day phase is just talking.

### 2. The Chameleon

**Players:** 3-8 | **Time:** 10-15 minutes | **Complexity:** Very Low

**How It Works:**
- A topic card is shown to everyone (e.g., a grid of 16 words related to "Animals").
- Everyone EXCEPT the Chameleon knows which specific word on the grid is the secret word.
- Going around the table, each player says ONE word as a clue related to the secret word.
- The Chameleon must fake it — give a clue that sounds plausible without knowing the actual word.
- After clues, everyone discusses and votes on who the Chameleon is.
- If caught, the Chameleon gets one last chance to guess the secret word. If they guess right, they escape.

**What Makes It Brilliant:**
- **Dual tension for EVERYONE.** Non-chameleons must give clues specific enough to prove they know the word, but vague enough that the Chameleon can't figure it out. Too specific = you help the Chameleon. Too vague = you look like the Chameleon.
- **No night phase at all.** The entire game is one conversation.
- **Incredibly simple to teach.** "Everyone knows a word except one person. Give clues. Find the faker."
- **The endgame guess** creates a second layer — even if you catch the Chameleon, they can still win.

**Why It Works for Recovery Adaptation:**
- Recovery word grid instead of generic topics ("What part of recovery is the secret word?")
- The tension of "prove you know without giving too much away" mirrors recovery themes of vulnerability and authenticity
- Extremely simple rules — can be taught in 30 seconds
- Zero components needed beyond a phone screen showing the grid

**Phone Feasibility:** VERY HIGH. This is essentially what Sacred Imposter already is. The phone shows the word grid to each player (or just the word), the Chameleon sees "you're the Chameleon" + the category. The rest is verbal.

**NOTE:** Sacred Imposter's core "Imposter" game is already a streamlined version of this mechanic — one word instead of a grid, one-word clues in a circle, vote to find the imposter. The Chameleon's "grid of words" variant could be a future game mode.

### 3. Insider (Oink Games)

**Players:** 4-8 | **Time:** 15 minutes | **Complexity:** Low

**How It Works:**
- One player is the **Master** (knows the secret word, answers yes/no questions).
- One player is the **Insider** (secretly knows the word, tries to subtly guide the group to guess it).
- Everyone else is a **Common** (asks questions, tries to guess the word).
- 5-minute timer: everyone asks yes/no questions to figure out the word.
- If the word is guessed, the group then has to figure out WHO was the Insider — who was steering the conversation?

**What Makes It Brilliant:**
- **Two-phase deduction.** First you solve the word puzzle together. Then you solve the social puzzle.
- **The Insider's dilemma:** Guide too obviously and you'll be caught. Guide too subtly and the word won't be guessed (and everyone loses).
- **Cooperative AND competitive** — everyone works together on the word, then turns on each other.
- **Word-guessing is inherently fun** — even without the deduction layer, 20-questions is a good time.

**Why It Works for Recovery Adaptation:**
- Recovery-themed secret words create natural therapeutic conversation
- The yes/no question format builds engagement even for quieter group members
- The Insider mechanic adds a "who's being genuine?" layer that maps to recovery themes of honesty
- Very phone-friendly — phone holds the word, timer, and role assignments

**Phone Feasibility:** HIGH. Phone assigns roles (pass-around reveal), shows the word to Master and Insider, runs a timer for the question phase. All gameplay is verbal.

### 4. Werewords

**Players:** 4-10 | **Time:** 10 minutes | **Complexity:** Low

**How It Works:**
- Combines One Night Werewolf's hidden roles with a word-guessing game.
- The **Mayor** knows the secret word and answers yes/no questions.
- The **Seer** also knows the word and tries to subtly help the group guess it.
- The **Werewolf** knows the word and tries to subtly derail the guessing.
- Everyone asks yes/no questions to guess the word within a time limit.
- If the word IS guessed: Werewolf can still win by correctly identifying the Seer.
- If the word is NOT guessed: Village can still win by correctly identifying the Werewolf.

**What Makes It Brilliant:**
- **Active sabotage.** The Werewolf is actively trying to mislead, not just hiding.
- **Everyone participates.** No one sits out. Everyone is asking questions.
- **Three win conditions** keep the tension high even after the word is guessed or missed.
- **Has a free app** that manages roles, word selection, and the timer.

**Why It Works for Recovery Adaptation:**
- The "someone is trying to derail your progress" metaphor is potent for recovery groups
- Word guessing creates low-pressure engagement
- The Seer/Werewolf dynamic — one person helping, one person sabotaging — models real recovery dynamics
- Official app handles all game management

**Phone Feasibility:** VERY HIGH. The official Werewords app already works on a single phone. Roles are assigned via the app, the word is shown during a pass-around, then it's all verbal.

### 5. Spyfall

**Players:** 3-8 | **Time:** 10-15 minutes | **Complexity:** Very Low

**How It Works:**
- Everyone gets a card showing the same **location** (e.g., "a hospital") except one player — the **Spy** — who doesn't know the location.
- Players take turns asking each other questions about the location.
- Non-spies must give answers that prove they know the location without making it obvious.
- The Spy must bluff convincingly and try to figure out the location from context.
- At any time, anyone can accuse someone of being the Spy (majority vote).
- The Spy can also guess the location at any time to win instantly.

**What Makes It Brilliant:**
- **Hilarious question dynamics.** "How do you feel about the uniform?" at a hospital vs. at a military base.
- **Universal participation.** Everyone is actively interrogating or being interrogated.
- **No moderator, no night phase, no elimination.**
- **Simple enough for anyone.** "Ask questions. Don't reveal the location. Find the spy."

**Why It Works for Recovery Adaptation:**
- Instead of locations, use **recovery situations/scenarios** ("AA meeting," "family visit," "job interview in recovery," "first sober weekend")
- The question-and-answer format naturally generates therapeutic conversation
- The Spy's experience of "not knowing what everyone else knows" resonates with early recovery feelings
- Very low barrier to entry

**Phone Feasibility:** HIGH. Phone shows each player their location card (pass-around). There are already several Spyfall apps that work on a single phone.

### 6. Coup

**Players:** 2-6 | **Time:** 15 minutes | **Complexity:** Low

**How It Works:**
- Each player has 2 face-down influence cards (from a deck of 5 character types: Duke, Assassin, Captain, Ambassador, Contessa).
- On your turn, you claim to BE any character and use their power (collect taxes as Duke, steal as Captain, assassinate, etc.).
- **You can claim ANY character whether you have it or not.**
- Anyone can call your bluff ("I don't think you're the Duke").
- If challenged and you were bluffing, you lose a card. If you were telling the truth, the challenger loses a card.
- Lose both cards, you're out. Last player standing wins.

**What Makes It Brilliant:**
- **Pure bluffing.** There are no hidden actions — it's all about claiming things and deciding whether to believe people.
- **Every turn is a decision.** Claim something safe? Claim something aggressive? Call someone out?
- **Very fast.** Games are often 5-10 minutes.
- **Simple enough to learn in 2 minutes.** Only 5 character types to know.

**Why It Works for Recovery Adaptation:**
- Re-skin the characters as recovery roles (Sponsor, Counselor, Old-Timer, Newcomer, etc.)
- The bluffing mechanic creates a "fake it till you make it" theme that's very on-the-nose for recovery
- Calling bluffs = holding each other accountable
- Very fast rounds keep energy high

**Phone Feasibility:** MEDIUM. Could work with pass-around to see your cards, but the game has ongoing card reveals and challenges that are tricky on one phone. Would need some adaptation. Better suited as a physical card game or a game where the phone just tracks who has what.

## Tier 2: Strong Mechanics Worth Borrowing (But Need More Adaptation)

---

### 7. A Fake Artist Goes to New York

**Players:** 5-10 | **Time:** 15-20 minutes | **Complexity:** Very Low

**How It Works:**
- Everyone draws on a shared piece of paper. Everyone knows the subject except the **Fake Artist.**
- Each player adds ONE continuous line/stroke per turn (two rounds of drawing).
- The Fake Artist tries to blend in without knowing what they're supposed to draw.
- After drawing, everyone votes on who the Fake Artist is.
- If the Fake Artist is caught but can guess the subject, they still win.

**Core Mechanic to Borrow:** The "contribute to a shared activity while one person fakes it" pattern. This could be adapted without drawing — e.g., everyone shares a recovery-related sentence or story about a topic, and the imposter has to fake their way through.

**Phone Feasibility:** LOW for drawing version (needs paper). HIGH for a verbal adaptation.

---

### 8. Deception: Murder in Hong Kong

**Players:** 4-12 | **Time:** 20 minutes | **Complexity:** Medium

**How It Works:**
- One player is the **Forensic Scientist** (moderator-like role, cannot speak, only gives clues).
- One player is the **Murderer** (chose a weapon and evidence card from their hand).
- Everyone else is an **Investigator** trying to identify the correct weapon + evidence combo.
- The Forensic Scientist communicates ONLY by placing markers on category tiles (like "Cause of Death: Suffocation" or "Relationship to Victim: Stranger").

**Core Mechanic to Borrow:** The "silent clue-giver" role is powerful. One person communicates only through constrained hints. The group must interpret. This could become a recovery version where a "Counselor" role communicates recovery concepts through limited word tiles while the group tries to understand what situation is being described.

**Phone Feasibility:** MEDIUM. The clue tiles would need to be on the phone, and the clue-giver would need the phone while others watch. Workable but not ideal for pass-around.

---

### 9. Secret Hitler / Secret ___

**Players:** 5-10 | **Time:** 30-45 minutes | **Complexity:** Medium

**How It Works:**
- Players are secretly Liberals or Fascists. One Fascist is "Hitler."
- Each round: elect a President and Chancellor. They draw policy cards and enact one.
- Liberals want 5 liberal policies or to kill Hitler. Fascists want 6 fascist policies or to elect Hitler as Chancellor.
- Passing fascist policies grants the President special powers (investigate someone, execute someone).
- The dilemma: the policy deck is stacked with more fascist cards, so even honest liberals might be forced to pass bad policies.

**Core Mechanic to Borrow:** The "forced bad outcome" mechanic is genius. Even when two honest players form the government, the card draw might force them to pass a fascist policy — creating genuine doubt about their loyalty. In a recovery context: even someone trying their best might "slip" due to circumstances, and the group has to decide if it was intentional or situational.

**Phone Feasibility:** LOW-MEDIUM. Too many simultaneous components (policy cards, election tracker, power unlocks) for a pass-around phone. Would need significant simplification.

---

### 10. The Resistance / Avalon

**Players:** 5-10 | **Time:** 20-30 minutes | **Complexity:** Low-Medium

**How It Works:**
- Players are secretly Resistance or Spies.
- Over 5 rounds, a leader proposes a team to go on a "mission." Everyone votes to approve/reject the team.
- Approved teams go on the mission: team members secretly play Success or Fail cards.
- If ANY Fail card is played, the mission fails. The group knows someone on that team was a spy — but not who.
- 3 successful missions = Resistance wins. 3 failed missions = Spies win.

**Avalon addition:** Special roles — **Merlin** (knows who the spies are but must be subtle) and **Assassin** (if the good team wins, the Assassin gets one chance to identify Merlin; if correct, evil wins).

**Core Mechanic to Borrow:** The mission-based structure. Instead of voting to eliminate someone, you're voting on teams and seeing results. This creates CONCRETE INFORMATION — "that mission failed, so one of those 3 people is a spy" — unlike classic Mafia where you have nothing to go on.

**Phone Feasibility:** MEDIUM. The team-vote and mission-card mechanics would need clever phone UI but could work.

---

### 11. Skull (Skull & Roses)

**Players:** 3-6 | **Time:** 15-30 minutes | **Complexity:** Very Low

**How It Works:**
- Each player has 4 discs: 3 roses and 1 skull.
- Each round: everyone places one disc face-down. Then players take turns either placing another disc or making a BID (a number of discs they think they can flip without hitting a skull).
- Bidding goes up until everyone passes. The winner must flip that many discs — starting with their OWN discs first, then choosing from others.
- Flip a skull = you lose one of your discs permanently. Successfully flip your bid = you score a point.
- First to 2 points wins.

**Core Mechanic to Borrow:** The "commit first, then challenge" pattern. Everyone puts something face-down, then someone has to back their confidence with action. The mandatory "flip your own first" rule means you can bluff by placing your skull and then bidding high — forcing others to either outbid you (and risk your skull) or let you win.

**Phone Feasibility:** MEDIUM. Could work with phone showing each person's "play" secretly, but the bidding round is very interactive and fast-paced — might be clunky on one phone.

---

### 12. Wavelength

**Players:** 2-12+ | **Time:** 30 minutes | **Complexity:** Very Low

**How It Works:**
- A spectrum card shows two extremes (e.g., "Underrated ←→ Overrated").
- One player (the Psychic) sees where a hidden target sits on that spectrum.
- The Psychic gives ONE clue that represents where the target is (e.g., if the target is 70% toward "Overrated," they might say "Beyonce").
- The team discusses and places their guess on the spectrum.
- Score based on how close you get.

**Core Mechanic to Borrow:** Spectrum-based discussion is INCREDIBLE for group therapy. "Where does 'asking for help' fall on the spectrum from 'Weakness ←→ Strength'?" This generates rich, nuanced conversation naturally.

**Phone Feasibility:** HIGH. Phone shows the spectrum, one player gives a clue, group discusses. No hidden information needed beyond the target position.

**NOTE:** This isn't social deduction at all — it's a cooperative communication game. But the mechanic is so perfectly suited for recovery group discussions that it deserves inclusion as a future Sacred Imposter game mode.

## Tier 3: Good to Know About (Less Relevant for This Project)

### Blood on the Clocktower
- **Players:** 5-20+ | **Time:** 30-90 minutes
- Brilliant game but too complex. Requires an experienced Storyteller who actively manages the game. Dead players stay involved (ghost votes). The Storyteller can LIE to players. Drunk/poisoned mechanics mean your information might be wrong.
- **Mechanic worth noting:** Dead players still participate. This solves elimination but adds complexity.
- **Phone feasibility:** Very low. Too many moving parts.

### Two Rooms and a Boom
- **Players:** 6-30 | **Time:** 15 minutes
- Players split into two physical rooms, trade hostages between rounds. Red team wants Bomber near President; Blue team wants them apart.
- **Mechanic worth noting:** The "card-sharing" mechanic — players can choose to show their full card or just the team color. This "partial information reveal" creates trust dynamics.
- **Phone feasibility:** Low. Requires two physical rooms.

### Love Letter
- **Players:** 2-4 | **Time:** 15 minutes
- Ultra-simple card game with only 16 cards. Draw one, play one, use the power.
- **Mechanic worth noting:** Extreme minimalism. Proves a great game can be built from almost nothing.
- **Phone feasibility:** Medium. Could work as pass-and-play.

### Don't Mess with Cthulhu
- **Players:** 4-8 | **Time:** 15 minutes
- Players have face-down investigation cards. Take turns revealing one card from another player's set. Find all Elder Signs before Cthulhu is revealed.
- **Mechanic worth noting:** The "I'll look at one of YOUR cards" mechanic creates interpersonal dynamics. You're choosing to trust someone by looking at their card.
- **Phone feasibility:** Medium.

## Recommended Next Games for Sacred Imposter (Beyond the Imposter Game)

Based on this research, here are the mechanics most worth building as additional game modes, ranked by phone-feasibility and recovery-group fit:

### Priority 1: Recovery Wavelength (Spectrum Game)
- Show a spectrum (e.g., "Easy ←→ Hard" or "Weakness ←→ Strength")
- One player sees a target position and gives a clue
- Group discusses and guesses
- **Why:** Generates the richest therapeutic conversation of any mechanic here. Zero deception required. Works perfectly on one phone.

### Priority 2: Recovery Insider / Werewords Hybrid
- One player knows the secret recovery word, answers yes/no questions
- One hidden "Insider" also knows and subtly guides
- One hidden "Saboteur" knows and subtly misleads
- Group guesses the word, then hunts the Saboteur
- **Why:** Combines word-guessing fun with social deduction. Everyone participates verbally. Perfect for a phone timer + role assignment.

### Priority 3: Recovery Spyfall
- Everyone gets the same recovery scenario except the outsider
- Players ask each other questions about the scenario
- The outsider bluffs and tries to figure out the scenario
- **Why:** Natural therapeutic conversation emerges from the question format. Very simple to implement.

### Priority 4: Recovery One Night
- Roles assigned via phone (pass-around)
- One night phase with 2-3 simple roles that swap or peek
- Short discussion, then vote
- **Why:** The identity-swapping mechanic is the most unique and exciting thing in this design space. More complex to build but very rewarding.

## Key Design Principles Extracted

1. **No elimination.** Every successful modern variant has solved this. Dead time kills engagement.
2. **Everyone has a role.** Even "Villager" should feel like a role, not an absence of one.
3. **Short rounds, many plays.** 10-minute rounds let you play 5-6 games in an hour. Failure is low-stakes.
4. **Discussion IS the game.** The best social deduction games are 80% conversation, 20% mechanics.
5. **The phone is the moderator.** Removes the need for someone to sit out and manage the game.
6. **Dual tension is key.** The best mechanics put BOTH sides under pressure (Chameleon must fake it, but non-Chameleons must not reveal too much).
7. **Information creates engagement.** Games that give players something concrete to reason about (mission results, card swaps, clue patterns) are more engaging than pure guessing.
8. **Vulnerability through mechanics.** The best recovery adaptation will use game mechanics to create moments where players naturally open up — not forced vulnerability, but vulnerability that emerges from gameplay.
